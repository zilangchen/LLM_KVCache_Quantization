\begin{abstracten}

As the context windows of large language models scale from thousands
to hundreds of thousands of tokens, KV-cache memory grows linearly
with sequence length and has become the primary bottleneck for
long-context inference. INT4 quantization can in principle reduce
KV-cache memory by a factor of four, yet methods calibrated against
\emph{numerical reconstruction error} (MSE / percentile clipping)
are unstable in the long-context regime, and most existing work
treats \emph{calibration} and \emph{budget allocation} as two
independent optimization problems without a unifying organizing
principle.

This thesis proposes a \textbf{behavior-guided} quantization
framework in which attention \emph{behavior}---defined as the
\emph{joint} preservation of the attention distribution $a$
\emph{and} the attention output $o$---serves as the single
organizing objective. A telescoping decomposition of the output
error shows that $K$-errors distort $a$ through softmax while
$V$-errors shift $o$ through weighted aggregation; the two paths
are \emph{coupled}, not orthogonal. Consequently, preserving the
distribution $a$ also constrains the $\hat{a}_i$ factor inside the
aggregation-side term, which is why we use the \textbf{distribution-side
KL divergence} as an optimizable engineering proxy that bounds
both error paths. The framework then organizes calibration and
layer-wise budget allocation as two decisions sharing the same
behavior sensitivity profile. We validate the
framework on six open-source LLMs---Qwen2.5-\{1.5B, 3B, 7B, 14B\},
LLaMA-3.1-8B, and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3---across five LongBench
tasks under a clean-provenance protocol (pin=\code{ddada19}), and
summarize three contributions.

\textbf{First} (C1, framework): we present behavior preservation
as a single principle that connects calibration and allocation as
two decisions sharing the same sensitivity profile, grounded in an
attention error decomposition that attributes output error to (i)
$K$-error propagating through softmax to distort the attention
distribution and (ii) $V$-error propagating through weighted
aggregation to shift the output.

\textbf{Second} (C2, method instances): the framework closes a loop
at two bit-widths. The INT8 canonical path achieves empirical
indistinguishability from FP16 on Qwen2.5-1.5B (mean $\Delta{=}{+}0.02$
over three LongBench tasks). INT4-RoleAlign, built on the same
per-channel-Key/per-token-Value asymmetric format as KIVI but with
offline KL-based calibration replacing runtime absmax/min, attains
100\% Needle retrieval and $|\Delta \text{PPL}| \leq 0.15$ across
four models relative to KIVI. AutoK, a profile-guided coverage-based
budget proposer (cov$80\%$), emerges as a natural extension of the
framework and delivers the strongest single-family positive case on
Mistral-7B (wins 3 of 5 tasks).

\textbf{Third} (C3, empirical insight): a cross-model comparison
within a same-order INT4 budget band reveals a
\textbf{(family, scale, task)-dependent operating regime map}.
Qwen2.5-3B exhibits a small-scale early-layer rescue regime
(BA-$k_1$ outperforms heuristic-$k_1$ on NarrativeQA by roughly
$+4$ points); Qwen2.5-14B exhibits a large-scale top-tier regime
(top-3 policies lie within $3.5\%$ relative gap); Mistral-7B exhibits
an AutoK-dominant regime; heuristic position-based allocation
coincides with behavior-guided allocation in several regimes and
thereby stands as a strong baseline---this is a structural
observation rather than a methodological failure. The framework's
value therefore shifts from "beating a weak baseline" to
\textbf{"naming structural regimes"}: it provides a readout tool
that lets heterogeneous behavior patterns across models, scales,
and tasks be identified and reused, rather than a single globally
optimal point estimate.

\end{abstracten}
