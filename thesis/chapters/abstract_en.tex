\begin{abstracten}

As the long-context inference window of large language models continues to expand, the memory footprint of the key-value cache in autoregressive decoding grows linearly with sequence length. In real deployment settings, the decoding bottleneck therefore shifts beyond computation toward the increasing storage and memory-access cost of the KV cache. Reducing cache bit-width directly lowers memory footprint, but tensor-level reconstruction-error calibration commonly used in existing quantization paths may not fully characterize the functional deviation that appears once the quantized cache enters attention. Key-side perturbations can alter logits and reshape softmax attention distributions, while Value-side perturbations primarily affect output representations through weighted aggregation. Motivated by this propagation difference, this thesis proposes a behavior-aligned KV-cache quantization framework for efficient inference, using attention distributions, aggregation outputs, and task behavior as shared calibration and auditing targets that connect quantization-parameter selection, low-bit recovery, and layer-wise budget allocation.

This thesis first builds an INT8 baseline path with attention-distribution KL as the quantization calibration target, in order to test the fidelity of behavior-guided calibration under a conservative bit-width. It then introduces \texttt{INT4-RoleAlign}, which uses asymmetric per-channel Key and per-token Value formats to separately characterize Key-side attention-ranking shifts and Value-side output perturbations, rather than reducing the two propagation mechanisms to a single reconstruction objective. The offline calibration artifacts are further aggregated into layer-wise behavioral sensitivity profiles and continue to support behavior-guided fixed-$k$ allocation, positional heuristic comparison, and \texttt{AutoK} coverage-based budget proposals. The same set of behavioral readings thereby connects calibration, low-bit recovery, and budget selection into a continuous evidence chain, allowing the readings to both explain quantization-parameter selection and translate into layer-wise budget proposals.

Experiments across six open-source instruction models from the Qwen2.5, LLaMA-3.1, and Mistral families show clear family-, scale-, and task-dependent behavior in KV-cache quantization and budget allocation. The INT8 baseline path keeps task readings close to FP16 at a conservative bit-width, indicating that attention-distribution KL can provide a stable behavioral fidelity anchor for calibration. After moving to INT4, the instability of symmetric quantization mainly appears as Key-side low-bit noise that triggers attention-ranking shifts; the issue is not merely the reduction in bit-width, but the fact that the perturbation enters a more sensitive computation path. With a role-aware per-channel Key and per-token Value format, low-bit retrieval behavior can return to a usable regime. Cross-model allocation further shows that layer-wise sensitivity does not have a universal shape. The proposed \texttt{AutoK} translates behavioral sensitivity profiles into model-level budget candidates and provides effective selection on models with clear profile structure; early-layer protection, positional heuristics, and uniform allocation each fall into different applicability regimes. Allocation should therefore be treated as candidate selection jointly constrained by model family, scale, and task condition, rather than a fixed rule operating outside these conditions. System readings show that the INT4 path provides stable and predictable cache-capacity benefits, whereas fused-decode time gains must be judged together with $H_{kv}$, sequence length, and backend implementation. Overall, this thesis constructs a behavior-aligned KV-cache quantization framework centered on attention-behavior preservation, with K/V role-aware recovery as the low-bit pathway and \texttt{AutoK} together with behavioral sensitivity profiles as the budget-allocation interface.

\end{abstracten}
