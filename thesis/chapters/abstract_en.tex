\begin{abstracten}

As large language model context windows scale from thousands
to hundreds of thousands of tokens,
key-value (KV) cache memory grows linearly with sequence length
and has become the primary bottleneck for long-context inference deployment.
Low-bit quantization offers the most attractive compression---INT4 can
theoretically reduce KV cache to 1/4 of its original size---yet existing
methods calibrated with numerical reconstruction objectives
cannot explain the catastrophic failure of symmetric INT4 at long contexts:
Needle retrieval drops from 100\% to 0\% and perplexity degrades by over 15\%.

Our key insight is that KV cache quantization should preserve
attention behavior rather than minimize numerical reconstruction error.
We propose attention-KL as a unified calibration objective
and diagnostic lens:
as a calibration objective, it searches for quantization parameters
that minimize attention behavior deviation;
as a diagnostic lens, it reveals failure modes and structural root causes
across quantization configurations.

This principle ultimately yields the core contribution of this work---INT4-RoleAlign,
a diagnosis-derived role-aware low-bit design employing
differentiated asymmetric quantization axes
(per-channel Key + per-token Value)
with behavior-aligned percentile calibration.
INT4-RoleAlign restores Needle retrieval from 0\% to 100\%
across three models (Qwen2.5-1.5B/7B and LLaMA-3.1-8B)
at 73\% KV cache compression.
However, PPL degradation varies with model scale (8B: 2.4\%, 7B: 6.1\%, 1.5B: 13.7\%),
revealing that local information retrieval is substantially more robust
to INT4 quantization noise than global language modeling quality.

The design of INT4-RoleAlign follows a complete evidence chain.
The principle is first validated on INT8---behavior-aligned calibration
preserves quality while reducing KV cache memory by approximately 44\%
across three model families;
through a Triton fused kernel, it further reduces decode latency by 8--38\%
relative to the unfused INT8 baseline,
establishing the reference baseline for low-bit diagnosis.
The same diagnostic lens then reveals the structural root cause
of symmetric INT4 failure---controlled K/V ablation consistently identifies
Key quantization as the dominant quality loss factor
with pronounced GQA architecture dependence---directly
motivating the role-aware design of INT4-RoleAlign.

Beyond this primary design, the diagnostic framework also surfaces
an unexpected structural byproduct:
per-head temperature correction $\tau^{-1}$ (inv\_tau) exhibits strong
scale-dependence on GQA heads, improving PPL by 1.6\% at $H_{kv}=2$
but degrading by 3.4--6.0\% at $H_{kv} \geq 4$.
This regularity is consistent with a GQA noise-dilution intuition
($\sigma_{\text{eff}} \propto \sigma / \sqrt{N_{\text{rep}}}$,
under a simplifying assumption of approximately independent query-head noise)
and illustrates the structural-byproduct value of diagnostic frameworks:
beyond their primary calibration role, they can surface architectural
constraints that would otherwise remain implicit.

Our results demonstrate that behavior-aligned calibration
is not only a better quantization objective,
but also reveals failure root causes, derives
role-aware low-bit designs, and delineates their capability boundaries,
completing the principle-to-diagnosis-to-design-to-boundary closed loop.

\end{abstracten}
